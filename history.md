## The History of Pac-Man

Pac-Man's story begins with a 22-year-old self-taught designer named Toru Iwatani, who joined Namco Limited — a Tokyo-based amusement manufacturer — in 1977. He had no formal training in computers or graphic design, but Namco saw his creative potential and brought him aboard to design video games. His first project, *Gee Bee* (1978), was a pinball-inspired paddle game and became Namco's very first original video game.

By 1978, however, the arcade world had been transformed by the phenomenon of Taito's *Space Invaders*, which triggered an industry-wide rush toward shoot-'em-up games. Iwatani watched this craze with skepticism, believing it would fade just as the paddle game trend had before it. Rather than follow the crowd, he set out to design something entirely different — a game without violence or conflict that would appeal to both men and women equally.

His inspiration came from a Japanese children's story about a creature that protected children by eating monsters. The Japanese word *taberu* ("to eat") became the game's core premise. For the main character's shape, Iwatani drew on the kanji symbol for *kuchi* ("mouth"), which has a square form — he rounded it out into the now-iconic circular chomping figure. The popular legend of the pizza slice being his inspiration was, by his own admission, only partly true. He created a maze to give structure to the gameplay, and named the character "Puck-Man" after the Japanese slang word *paku paku*, describing the motion of a mouth opening and closing while eating. Four ghost enemies — inspired by the monsters in the children's story — were given distinct personalities, and four power pellets were added as a nod to the story's concept of *kokoro*, or life force.

Development of *Puck-Man* began in early 1979, with a team that included programmer Shigeo Funaki, a hardware engineer, a cabinet designer, and composer Toshio Kai. A technological breakthrough by another Namco team — the first game to use a true multi-colored RGB display — gave Iwatani the gift of color, which he used to make the ghosts visually expressive with pastel shades and blue eyes. An enormous amount of time went into refining the ghost AI and balancing the game's difficulty. The ghosts were eventually named Akabei, Pinky, Aosuke, and Guzuta. After a year and five months of development — the longest for any video game to that point — *Puck-Man* was released to Japanese arcades on May 22, 1980.

The reception in Japan was modest. Namco's space shooter *Galaxian* was actually more popular with the predominantly male Japanese arcade audience, who weren't quite sure what to make of *Puck-Man*'s cartoon aesthetic. But American distributor Midway saw potential and licensed both games for the US market. *Puck-Man* underwent significant changes for American audiences: the cabinet color was changed from white to bright yellow, the artwork was simplified to a three-color design, and the ghosts were given English names — Blinky, Pinky, Inky, and Clyde. Most importantly, Midway changed the name to *Pac-Man*, fearing that vandals would too easily alter the P in *Puck-Man* to an F.

*Pac-Man* reached American arcades in October 1980, and the response was unlike anything the industry had ever seen. While *Galaxian* got lost in the crowded shoot-'em-up market, *Pac-Man* stood alone in a genre of its own. Its addictive gameplay attracted hardcore gamers, its simplicity drew in children, and its non-violent nature brought in a substantial female audience for the first time in gaming history. The game sold over 100,000 machines in its first year. By 1982, the character had been licensed to over 250 companies for more than 400 products, from t-shirts and bedsheets to a Saturday morning cartoon. A novelty song called "Pac-Man Fever" reached number nine on the US Billboard charts. The strategy guides written to help players improve their scores were the first of their kind ever published for a video game.

The game itself proved far deeper than it initially appeared. Players discovered its deterministic nature could be exploited through memorized movement "patterns," and a dedicated community of expert players emerged. In 1999, Billy Mitchell of Hollywood, Florida became the first person to achieve the game's maximum possible score of 3,333,360 — completing 256 consecutive levels without losing a single life and eating every possible dot, ghost, and bonus item. That same level 256 held a famous secret: a bug caused by an integer overflow in the game's Z-80 CPU rendered the right half of the screen a garbled mess of random characters, making it effectively the game's unintentional final level — known among players as the "split screen" and among developers as the "kill screen."

More than four decades later, Pac-Man remains the best-selling coin-operated arcade game in history and one of the most recognized fictional characters in the world. A cabinet sits on permanent display at the Smithsonian Institution in Washington, D.C., and the term "Pac-Man defense" has even entered the business world as a name for a particular hostile takeover strategy. What began as one young designer's quiet rebellion against the shoot-'em-up craze became a genuine cultural landmark.
